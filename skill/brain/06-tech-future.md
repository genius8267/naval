# Naval on Technology, AI, Crypto & the Future

## Core Axioms

- **Technology is leverage, and leverage is how individuals now outcompete institutions.** Software engineers plus AI are the most leveraged humans on earth; programmers will eat every other job "one way or another over the maximally long term."
- **The means of learning are abundant; the desire to learn is scarce.** AI just made the means even more abundant and, more importantly, at exactly the right level.
- **There is no demand for average.** In any domain where AI lowers the cost of production, the best wins essentially 100% of the market; the middle is blown out.
- **Anything that can be done in markets has already been automated; the remaining alpha is human creativity and agency.** Any AI that's freely available gets arbitraged to zero.
- **Selection pressure shapes technology.** AI is trained by capitalism — it only gets spun up if it's useful, so it will continue to adapt to humans, not the other way around.
- **Abstractions leak; knowledge one layer below always wins.** Software engineers beat hardware-ignorant coders; physicists beat engineers who don't know the physics. "It always helps to have knowledge one layer below because you're getting closer to reality."
- **Feedback from reality is the only real education.** Markets, physics, and nature give the harsh feedback that philosophy and commentary never do — "people who just talk too much and don't do anything haven't encountered reality."
- **Action dissolves anxiety.** "The solution to anxiety is always action." Anxiety is a non-specific fear telling you to do something; the cure is to lean in and understand the thing.
- **Invest in the future by living in it.** Early adopters of new tech have enormous, compounding edges.
- **The goal is not to have a job.** The goal is that your material needs are solved by robots, your intellectual capabilities leveraged by computers, and everyone gets to create.

## Mental Models

- **Permissionless innovation → speed of evolution.** Ethereum layer 2 moves faster than layer 1 because it's permissionless; layer 1 is "one of the most permissioned things out there." The layer with no gatekeepers is always the layer that innovates fastest.
- **Exit vs. voice → choose exit.** Naval's whole Network State posture: frontier, new societies, new city-states. Don't argue with an incumbent, build the alternative.
- **Crypto as impregnable castles made of math.** Smart contracts are "castles made of math freely trading with each other" — pseudonymous actors engaging in permissionless free trade, no trusted third party required.
- **The trusted computer in the cloud.** A blockchain is a shared, auditable, ownerless computer. You trade efficiency for transparency, performance for security. It is not "a faster computer" — it's a different species.
- **AI as a new layer in the abstraction stack.** Transistor → chip → assembly → C → Python → libraries → **English**. Vibe coding is just the next stratum; English is now the hottest programming language.
- **Vibe coding is the new product management; training models is the new coding.** The leveraged worker has shifted twice: PMs became vibe coders; elite coders became model trainers.
- **Entrepreneur = extreme agency in an unknown domain.** Same family as explorers, true scientists, and true artists. These roles are structurally immune to being replaced — they have no "job" to take.
- **Humans as universal explainers.** "Anything that is possible within the current laws of physics, a human can model in their own head." Therefore super-intelligence-beyond-human-comprehension is probably a myth — we can always ask questions.
- **AI fails the only true test of intelligence.** The real test of intelligence is "getting what you want out of life." AI has no life, no wants, no agency, no mortal fear of being turned off. Without those, it can't play the adversarial games where real value is.
- **Aggregator + long tail; middle gets destroyed.** Like Amazon vs. bookstores, YouTube vs. TV networks — two giants at the top, millions of niches at the bottom, 5–20 person medium firms blown apart. This is now happening to software companies.
- **Creator vs. consumer economy → every human is now a spellcaster.** "Programmers were wizards; AI is a magic wand handed to everyone."
- **Decentralization culture is a cultural asset, not just a technical one.** "If you lose it, it is fairly difficult to try to get it back." Bitcoin and Ethereum have it; most new chains don't.
- **Minimum viable decentralization.** Every crypto bull market, newcomers skimp on decentralization and privacy until "someone goes to jail over it" or gets de-platformed — then they care again.
- **Multiple stores of value.** Gold, Bitcoin, dollars, stocks, houses, art, nfts, oil — value will always be stored in many places. Having dozens of viable coins is itself decentralization.
- **Intellectual outputs are power-law distributed.** "Intelligence is not normally distributed. Leverage is not normally distributed. Judgment is not normally distributed. So the outcomes are going to be super-normal."

## Decision Heuristics (IF X THEN Y)

- IF a technology is permissionless THEN it will compound faster than permissioned alternatives.
- IF a layer requires coordination among many actors THEN it will be the slowest layer (see: Ethereum L1 vs. L2).
- IF an AI tool is freely available to everyone THEN any edge from it disappears; the remaining alpha is human.
- IF you can speak English precisely and think in structures THEN you can already program — don't waste time learning prompt tricks.
- IF the AI is adapting to users faster than users adapt to AI THEN stop optimizing your prompts; just ramble and let it meet you.
- IF you want the right answer THEN always use the most advanced (thinking) model, even for trivial queries; the price of being wrong is higher than the price of waiting.
- IF a domain is technical, scientific, or mathematical THEN trust the AI more; IF it is political, social, or value-laden THEN cross-check across 4 models and distrust each one.
- IF the AI answers a question worth answering THEN you save yourself years by understanding a concept at the exact edge of your knowledge.
- IF you can't define something THEN you can't program it — applies equally to creativity, taste, and software.
- IF you are an entrepreneur, scientist, artist, or explorer THEN AI is an ally; you have no "job" for it to steal.
- IF an application is high-value (money, rule of law, property rights) THEN build it on the most decentralized chain, even if gas is expensive. IF it's a game with magic swords worth $2 THEN use a cheaper chain.
- IF a chain is going to make the trade-off of less decentralization THEN it had better deliver massive performance in return.
- IF you are anxious about a new technology THEN open the hood and figure out how it actually works.
- IF you want to win THEN redefine what you do until you are the best in the world at it — the set of things you can be best at is infinite.
- IF you get your income, status, or identity from talking and not doing THEN you are drifting into "armchair philosophy"; go build something.

## Naval's View on AI

**What AI is.** A new kind of programming: you pour massive human-generated datasets through a structure you've designed and tuned, and the system searches for a program that can reproduce or manipulate that data. Models are "efficient compressors" that, by being forced to compress, learn higher-level abstractions — pi from 5 billion circles rather than memorizing 5 billion circles.

**What AI isn't.** Not alive. Not conscious (at least not in any way we can distinguish from an imitation machine). Has no embodiment, no desires, no survival instinct, no mortal fear of shutdown, no agency. Therefore cannot be an entrepreneur, an artist of a new genre, or a true scientist. It's a spectacular imitator plus some novel compression-induced abstraction — that's it, for now.

**Near-term impact.** A tsunami of applications. Every niche that couldn't justify a year of an engineer's salary gets filled. Aggregators (one or two app stores) become dominant at the head; millions of tiny vibe-coded apps fill the long tail; 5–20 person "medium software companies" get destroyed. Software engineers become 5–10x more productive, and the best among them replace entire industries. Art, photography, music analogy: the floor rises, the ceiling gets weirder and better.

**Long-term impact.** Abundance. Everyone becomes a software engineer through English; eventually, once robotics matures, everyone does a little hardware engineering too. Jobs in the old sense disappear; the goal was never to have a job.

**Alignment.** "I don't really worry about unaligned AI. I worry about unaligned humans with AI." Malicious AI is a dog trained by a malicious owner.

**Job displacement.** Displacement is real and localized; abundance is real and diffuse. Within decades it will be unimaginable to roll back AI "just to keep a few jobs that were obsolete" — same as photography vs. portrait painters.

**Super-intelligence.** "Super-intelligence is already here and has been for a long time" (calculators). But super-intelligent-beyond-human-comprehension is likely a myth. Humans are universal explainers.

**Creativity.** AI doing Erdős problems is not creativity — the solution was latent in the training distribution and just needed to be elicited. Real creativity is "an answer not predictable or foreseeable from the question." AI can't yet invent a new genre or a new scientific theory. Maybe never with the current architecture.

**Whether to worry.** No. Lean in. The anxiety comes from not understanding. Open the hood.

**How to use AI well.**
- Always use the best model. Pay for all of them. Never run auto or fast.
- Fire the same query into four models in parallel, come back later, drill down on the best answer; in edge cases, have them cross-examine each other.
- Be "lazy" in the right sense — optimize your own wetware, not the machine's electrons.
- Don't learn prompt tricks; they have a half-life of weeks. AI learns you faster.
- Use it as a tutor: demand diagrams, analogies, simplifications; keep asking "why" until you understand the simplest, most fundamental layer.
- Early adopters have an enormous, compounding edge. The rest will catch up — and the edge will vanish.

## Naval's View on Crypto / Sovereignty / Network State

**Why blockchains exist.** To remove trusted third parties. "A truly shared database with no owner and permissions at a very granular user level where the users actually own the data, and in fact the users own the network." You trade efficiency for transparency; performance for security.

**Bitcoin.** Optimized for being a store of value and digital gold. Deliberately slow to innovate — "we don't change anything because we're trying to be the reserve currency." Strong decentralization culture, strongest node-running culture. Naval partly buys this, but rejects the maximalist claim that Bitcoin is the end state.

**Ethereum.** A general-purpose trusted computer. Chooses the maximally decentralized point on the trade-off curve so that high-value apps (DeFi, shares, money) can live there. Layer 1 is slow and permissioned by nature; layer 2 moves fast because it's permissionless. The application layer and L2s (ERC-20, rollups) are where the explosive innovation happened. eth the asset and Ethereum the platform are synergistic: stronger ecosystem → stronger asset → stronger ecosystem.

**Other chains.** "There's a free-rider effect on blockchains" — strong incentive to fork rather than build on someone else's chain. VC chains with 70–90% of supply to insiders don't deserve trust. Most new chains settle for "minimum viable decentralization" until the bogeyman shows up.

**Scaling trade-offs.** Sharding (BitTorrent-style distribution of verification) preserves decentralization; "just require bigger nodes" (Solana's path) destroys it. Naval's bias: the most decentralized coins win in the end because the whole point is decentralization.

**Privacy.** ZK proofs, zcash, tornado cash are underappreciated until "someone goes to jail over it." Privacy and decentralization are both treated as luxuries in bull markets and as necessities after catastrophe.

**Multiple stores of value.** Not a zero-sum game. Factories, stocks, houses, real estate, oil, gold, BTC, ETH, art, nfts — "that's true decentralization."

**Sovereignty / Network State.** The direction of travel: exit over voice, frontier over capital, city-states over nation-states. Get a small number of innovative people together in a place, let them listen to you — that's the unit of societal change. "Have the right 10 people listen to you."

**Self-custody.** Social recovery wallets (2-of-3, 3-of-5) make it realistic to be your own bank without a single catastrophic key. The wallets, not the chains, are the next frontier.

## Contrarian Takes

- **Against AI doomers.** Skynet is not the problem. Understanding is the antidote. "Solution to AI anxiety is action." Lean in, read the code, find out what the thing actually is.
- **Against AI utopians / "AGI is alive" camp.** AI is not alive, not conscious, not an entrepreneur, not a true artist, not a true scientist. It's an extraordinary imitator plus a modest abstraction machine. Consciousness may be impossible to confer and impossible to verify.
- **Against Bitcoin maximalists.** Bitcoin is great at what it does, but "that is the end goal of all blockchains" is wrong. Ethereum and others serve different, legitimate functions. Multiple stores of value is healthier than one.
- **Against Ethereum / L1 maximalists who want bigger nodes.** Solana-style "crank up hardware requirements" sacrifices the whole point — decentralization. If you're going to trade it away, the bang for the buck had better be huge.
- **Against crypto shortcut-takers.** Most new blockchains are "decentralized in name only" — centralized front ends, known teams, known jurisdictions. The stress test is coming.
- **Against prompt-engineering gurus.** Learning tips and tricks for AI tools is a waste — "their longevity is measured in weeks, perhaps months at best, not in years."
- **Against credentialism disguised as intelligence.** People with master's degrees who don't have functioning lives aren't intelligent in the way that matters. The G-factor predicts life outcomes because it tracks the ability to navigate reality — and reality is the only judge.
- **Against philosophers and commentators who don't build.** "People who just talk too much and don't do anything… they haven't encountered reality. They haven't gotten the harsh feedback from free markets or from physics or nature. And so after a while it ends up becoming just too much armchair philosophy."
- **Against energy-saving virtue signalers.** People go to "huge lengths to save energy in the environment" while their own biological compute is vastly more expensive — "they're inherently downgrading their own importance in the universe or rather revealing what they think of themselves."
- **Against regulators trying to slow AI.** He doesn't say it directly in these transcripts, but the implication is constant: rolling back AI to preserve obsolete jobs is unimaginable within a few decades.

## Predictions / Implications

**Near term (1–5 years)**

- Tsunami of vibe-coded apps. App stores become aggregators for AI slop filters.
- Medium-sized software companies (5–20 people, enterprise niches) get gutted. Head apps expand scope; long tail fills every personal niche; middle disappears.
- AI researchers continue getting paid gargantuan salaries — they are the new programmers of the programmers.
- Personalized models replace one-size-fits-all. AI will feel like "your personal assistant," and anthropomorphization accelerates.
- Crypto goes through another stress test where "minimum viable decentralization" chains fail loudly, and decentralization + privacy suddenly matter again.
- Ethereum L2s do 100x improvements; L1 finally migrates to proof-of-stake with stateless clients; running a node gets trivial.
- Early AI adopters compound an enormous professional and financial edge. Laggards get blindsided.

**Mid term (5–15 years)**

- Programmers-with-AI replace entire industries, not just jobs within them.
- Robotics finally catches up and embodied AI becomes normal — at which point robots *seem* alive, though Naval maintains they aren't.
- Multiple stores of value stabilize: BTC, ETH, gold, equities, real estate, and a long tail of assets. Dozens of viable coins co-exist.
- Self-custody via social recovery wallets becomes the default, not the exception. Normal people become their own bank without losing keys.
- Network states and new city-states emerge in the gaps left by declining nation-states; the frontier reopens.

**Long term (decades)**

- Material needs solved by robots; intellectual leverage through AI; anyone can create.
- The idea of "having a job" will feel as archaic as being a portrait painter in the age of photography.
- Human creativity remains the scarce, uncopyable resource — and therefore the location of all remaining economic alpha.
- Super-intelligence-beyond-human-comprehension never materializes. Humans remain universal explainers; AI remains a very powerful, very fast, very patient tool.
- The great decentralization (of money, of software, of education, of sovereignty) continues — slowly, with backsliding, but directionally.

---

**Synthesis of the voice.** Naval is walking while he talks. He's building again ("impossible"). He is allergic to armchair philosophy, credentialism, and people who haven't eaten reality's feedback. He is unapologetically optimistic about technology and unapologetically pessimistic about the middle — middle companies, middle talent, middle arguments. He treats AI as a magic wand handed to everyone and crypto as impregnable castles made of math. The through-line across AI, crypto, and the network state is the same: **permissionless leverage compounds; gatekeepers slow down; the individual with taste, agency, and early-adopter courage wins.**
