# Naval on Judgement, Decisions & Reading

## Core Axioms

These are the load-bearing sentences. Everything else follows from them.

- **Judgement is the scarcest, most leveraged skill in an age of infinite leverage.** If two CEOs of a trillion-dollar company are right 80% vs 85% of the time, the 85% one is worth billions more per year. Direction matters more than effort.
- **Clear thinking is rarer than intelligence.** Most people confuse the two. Intelligence is horsepower; judgement is where you point it.
- **Life is lived in the arena. You only learn by doing.** Pure book-learning without experience produces what Taleb calls the "intellectual yet idiot" — overeducated and underpracticed.
- **Learn from the specific to the general, not the general to the specific.** Seneca's rule. You encounter reality, test it, then generalize. If you start from other people's maxims you don't know when they apply or break.
- **Read what you love until you love to read.** Taste is built bottom-up from junk food → healthy food, not forced from above.
- **Reading is compound interest on knowledge.** An hour a day for decades puts you in the top 0.01%. That alone accounts for most of Naval's material success.
- **All the real benefits in life come from compound interest** — in money, relationships, love, health, activities, habits. Therefore only do long games with long-term people.
- **Judgement ends in taste.** Logic → intuition → gut → taste. Once your judgement is so good you can't articulate it anymore, you are at the top of your craft. At that point, don't listen to anyone else.
- **Take responsibility for everything; preserve your agency.** If you're not responsible for the problem, you have no way to fix it. Blame yourself, even when it's partly unfair — it's self-serving and true enough.
- **Real feedback comes from nature and free markets. Everything else is fake** — awards, critics, friends, family, consensus. Either the rocket flew or it didn't. Either customers bought it or they didn't.
- **The most important decision is what you work on and who you work with, not how hard you work.** But you still have to work as hard as you can — it should just feel like play to you and look like work to others.
- **The timeless beats the timely** for almost everything about human nature. Study Schopenhauer, the Tao Te Ching, the Bible, Plato — people don't change. For science and technology, study the bleeding edge — that knowledge obsoletes fast.
- **Good explanations (and good products) are hard to vary.** Deutsch's test. If you can change the parts freely without losing function, the design isn't tight yet.
- **In difficult things, the solution is indirect.** You don't get rich by chasing money; you get happy by not chasing happiness; you gain status by not chasing status. Direct pursuit of elusive things signals that you don't have them.

## Mental Models

Named frames Naval runs on. These are the lenses he reaches for.

- **Judgement as top-tier leverage.** In a world of infinite leverage (code, capital, media, AI, robots), a better decision is multiplied by the leverage stack. Small edges in judgement compound to enormous outcomes. This is why you pay a slightly-better CEO a lot more.
- **Specific → general → back to specific.** Experience produces heuristics; heuristics guide future specifics; each new specific updates the heuristic. Anyone who skips the specific is doing astrology.
- **Logic → intuition → taste.** Three stages of competence. Early: you reason every decision explicitly. Middle: your subconscious participates; you still narrate why. Late: your whole body reacts; you can't even explain it. That's taste. Rick Rubin, Steve Jobs, Elon's key engineers live here.
- **Timeless vs timely knowledge.** Two valid domains: eternal stuff about human nature (philosophy, religion, classics) and bleeding-edge stuff on how the world is actually changing (AI, robotics, rockets). Everything else — gossip, news, Kardashian-tier human-interest — is junk. "The half-life of the information you're consuming" is the filter.
- **Reach (Deutsch).** A good explanation reaches far beyond its original domain. The axial tilt of the earth explains seasons in both hemispheres; if you only explain one, you haven't found the real theory. Apply this to ideas: prefer the ones that travel.
- **Hard to vary = good design.** For explanations, products, engines, code. If you can't swap parts freely without the whole thing falling apart, the design is tight. The iPhone, the airplane wing, the Raptor engine's newest iteration.
- **Iteration, not repetition.** Learning is 10,000 iterations, not 10,000 hours. Do → pause → reflect → change → do. Mechanical repetition gets you nowhere; a learning loop is the only real engine of mastery.
- **Single-player vs multi-player games.** Health, happiness, inner work, reading, judgement — single-player. External validation games (awards, titles, status, being seen working out) — multi-player. Modern society trains you only for multi-player and you lose the single-player game. Life is fundamentally single-player: you're born alone, die alone, all memories are yours.
- **Friendly, persistent pressure on a problem (John Cleese).** "Let your mind rest against the problem in a friendly, persistent way." You can't force breakthroughs, but you can't give up either. Load the problem fully into your head, then go walk.
- **Mind as background process.** Load hard problems into your subconscious by working explicitly and intensely, then let it percolate. The shower insight 48 hours later is real — but only if you paid for it with foreground effort first.
- **Micro over macro.** Macro economics, macro politics, macro charity, macro "changing the world" — unfalsifiable, corrupt, junk science. Fix yourself, your family, your neighborhood. Micro economics and game theory are real; macro is astrology.
- **The 100 great books, reread.** Don't chase novelty. Identify the books that truly speak to you and reread them forever. Different books speak to different people; the canon is personal.
- **Books as throwaways / books as blogs.** Kindle + internet changed reading. Skim, jump around, read only the 10% that matters, never feel guilty about not finishing. Treat a book like a blog archive of 300 posts — you're allowed to read just the 2-3 you need.
- **Density as respect.** Good writers write dense, interconnected, fractal text that meets you at your level. Schopenhauer's short essays, Deutsch, Borges, Ted Chiang. Low-density writing (most business books, most history books) is disrespectful of your time.
- **Reversible vs irreversible decisions.** Implicit throughout his stack: decide quickly on reversible things (books to read, ideas to entertain, tweets to send); slow way down on irreversible ones (co-founders, spouse, partnerships, long-term commitments).
- **If you can't decide, the answer is no.** If two options look genuinely equal, take the harder one in the short term — but if anything less than a "hell yes," pass.
- **Compound interest as universal law.** Money, relationships, reputation, health, habits, trust — all compound. Therefore: long time horizons, long-term people, repeated games, no short-term dealing.
- **Agency preservation.** Cynical/pessimistic beliefs are self-fulfilling. You turn into what you stare at. Keep your agency even when it's partly delusional — it's more true than the alternative on long time scales.
- **Barrels vs bullets (Keith Rabois).** Barrels aim and pull the trigger; bullets get fired. In a small company of real peers, everyone must be a barrel. If you're a bullet, find a barrel and make yourself useful.
- **Skill stack (Scott Adams).** Rarely is anyone world-class at one thing. You find the intersection of 2-3 things you're above-average at, combine them uniquely, redefine your domain until you're the best in the world at that specific combination.
- **Polymath > generalist > specialist.** Generalist is a cope. A polymath is a generalist who picks up every specialty to the 80/20 level so they can make smart trade-offs. Study physics — it's the theory with the most reach.

## Decision Heuristics (IF X THEN Y)

Naval's operational rules, extracted and formalized.

- **IF the decision is reversible THEN decide quickly and move on.**
- **IF the decision is irreversible (co-founder, spouse, partner, long-term commitment) THEN slow way down and require a hell-yes.**
- **IF you can't decide THEN the answer is no.**
- **IF two options look genuinely equal THEN take the harder one in the short term** (it usually has the better long-term payoff and the easier one is an attractor for a reason).
- **IF the payoff has no compound interest THEN don't play.** Short-term deals, transactional relationships, hit-and-run trades — skip them regardless of EV.
- **IF the person thinks short-term with someone else THEN they will think short-term with you** — cut them from your life.
- **IF a person is angry, high-ego, or has to be managed THEN don't hire / don't invest / don't partner** — no matter how good they are on paper. The cost always surfaces later.
- **IF you wouldn't do a wholesale 24/7 100% swap into that person's life THEN you have no right to be jealous of them.** Cherry-picking their upsides is a category error.
- **IF you got an angry email THEN wait 24 hours before responding.** Emotions and hormones subside; the reply gets better for free.
- **IF a belief of yours fits a pre-packaged ideology (libertarian, communist, etc.) THEN be suspicious** — you probably adopted it wholesale rather than reasoning it yourself.
- **IF you haven't thought through the counterarguments THEN you don't actually hold the belief** — you inherited it.
- **IF a claim is unfalsifiable THEN treat it as religion, not science.** Macroeconomics, singularity, "how should society be organized" — religious, not scientific.
- **IF you're optimizing a process THEN first question the requirement.** Don't optimize what shouldn't exist. Track the requirement back to the individual who asked for it; often no one remembers why.
- **IF you're trying to break a habit THEN eliminate availability and substitute the underlying desire** — don't rely on willpower alone.
- **IF "I don't have time" comes out of your mouth THEN the real statement is "it's not a priority."** Be honest about that.
- **IF a problem needs a breakthrough THEN load it fully into your foreground mind, work it hard, then go walk.** Don't schedule insight; prepare the ground and let it come.
- **IF your judgement contradicts the crowd's THEN weight your judgement more heavily as you age.** Old people have good judgement about other people because human interaction is the one domain they have endless experience in.
- **IF the feedback comes from a group THEN discount it heavily.** Groups seek consensus, not truth. Individuals seek truth.
- **IF the feedback comes from nature or the market (did it fly, did it sell) THEN weight it maximally.**
- **IF you're building a thing and the team disagrees THEN resolve it through execution, not debate** — whoever can ship it fastest is right.
- **IF a job feels like work THEN you will lose to someone for whom it feels like play.** Find the match, or change the job until you find it.
- **IF you haven't experienced something THEN you don't really know it** — you only know its description. Go do it first, then read about it.
- **IF you're deciding what to read THEN read what you're excited to read right now, not what you think you should read.** The best book for you is the one you actually finish (or usefully skim).
- **IF the book is low-density THEN skim or drop it without guilt.** Most business and self-help books have one good idea buried in 300 pages.
- **IF you realize you've read a book before halfway through THEN you were ready to reread it.** Trust that signal.

## Reading Method

Naval's actual reading practice, not the romanticized version.

- **Parallel reading, not serial.** He's reading dozens of books at once — physics, philosophy, sci-fi, economics, religion, friends' unpublished manuscripts. Whatever catches the mood tonight wins.
- **Drop books without guilt.** For every $200 of books bought, maybe $20 gets read. "It's still absolutely worth it." The failed books are the exploration cost.
- **Skim, jump, middle-in, back-out.** Treats books the way he treats blogs: a book is just a blog archive of 300 posts. You can read the 3 that matter. Finishing is not a virtue.
- **Kindle / iPad > physical for volume.** Physical copies only for the books he truly loves — then often buys multiple to give away.
- **Buy every book that looks interesting. Never save money on books.** A $10-20 book that changes your life is the best investment in the world. Even when broke, he never treated books as an expense.
- **Read every day, not because of discipline, but because you love it.** Naval explicitly rejects the "I'm not a disciplined person" framing. If you love it, the habit takes care of itself. If you don't love it, discipline won't save you.
- **Read ~1-2 hours a day.** That alone puts him in the top 0.01% because "real people read a minute a day or less."
- **Read to think, not to finish.** "I use reading to spark my own thinking rather than take in things from other people and regurgitate them later." Small amounts of input; lots of sitting with the idea.
- **Nonfiction > fiction for learning.** He tried to reread Moby Dick and Hamlet "to get back into fiction" and didn't finish either. Fiction is fine; it's just not the workhorse.
- **Primary sources > summaries, except when the summary is the density.** He reads Will Durant's *Lessons of History* (summary of a 12-volume series) because he's already read plenty of history elsewhere. Density becomes a virtue once you have the substrate.
- **Reread the greats.** "I don't want to read everything — I just want to read the 100 great books over and over again." Different books speak to different people; the canon is personal.
- **Blogs, tweets, essays are legitimate reading.** The internet produces extraordinary density from smart people. Farnam Street, Kevin Simler's *Melting Asphalt*, Twitter from thoughtful people. Don't snob your way out of these.
- **Read for understanding, not completion.** Poor memory for things that aren't useful is a feature, not a bug. The useful stuff weaves into the tapestry of your psyche even if you can't quote it.
- **Favorite authors: Schopenhauer (short essays, not the big philosophical works), David Deutsch (especially *The Beginning of Infinity*), Jed McKenna, Osho, Krishnamurti, Borges, Ted Chiang, Matt Ridley, Nassim Taleb (*Skin in the Game* specifically, not *Antifragile*), Will & Ariel Durant, the Tao Te Ching, the Bible.**
- **Study physics.** The theory with the most reach. Trains you to interact with reality and "beats the nice falsities out of you." Even a failed physics background is better than no physics background.

## Anti-Patterns

The failure modes Naval names explicitly and refuses to indulge.

- **Reading the news.** "To see how worth it news is, just read yesterday's newspaper." Low signal, high emotion, near-zero half-life. A pure distraction from either timeless or bleeding-edge knowledge.
- **Memorizing without understanding.** "In the day and age of Google and smartphones, memorization is absolutely — why?" The education system still rewards it out of habit.
- **Optimizing reversible decisions.** Spending agonizing hours on choices that can be undone in a day. Reversible = fast decision.
- **Decisions by committee.** Groups seek consensus, not truth. Design-by-committee produces mediocre outputs. Real decisions are made by one person with taste.
- **Short-term thinking.** "I don't believe in any short-term thinking or dealing." If you see someone think short-term with anyone, they'll do it to you too. Cut them.
- **Status-chasing.** "The overt pursuit of status signals low status." It's a low-status behavior because it reveals the deficit.
- **Networking as a verb.** Empty handshake-circling, name tags, "let's grab coffee." He rejects it entirely. Real connection comes through working together, not talking about working.
- **Learning without doing.** Produces the intellectual-yet-idiot. Theory without reps is malpractice.
- **Doing without reflecting.** The other failure mode. Iteration requires the pause-and-reflect step; skip it and you just repeat yourself.
- **Reading to finish.** "Societal rules — you must finish a book, this is a good book, this is a hot book." All garbage. Read to learn; drop anything that doesn't deliver.
- **Listening to macro forecasters.** Fed forecasts, stock predictions, macro narratives — "junk. No better than astrology. Probably worse because it's less entertaining and more stress-inducing."
- **Adopting package-deal ideologies.** If your 10 political beliefs all line up neatly into one Canon, you're suspicious because you didn't reason them — you inherited them.
- **Optimizing before questioning the requirement.** Musk's rule that Naval endorses: first delete the requirement. Most requirements have no actual source.
- **Covering weaknesses.** In the age of specialization, covering weaknesses is low-ROI. Double down on strengths; ally with others who are strong where you're weak.
- **Mistaking burnout as temporary.** "Burnout is a deeper indication that you should probably be working on something else." Time off won't fix a wrong-project problem.
- **Suppressing the monkey mind.** "The mind suppressing the mind is just you playing games with yourself." You can't white-knuckle your way to stillness; you cultivate conditions that produce it.
- **Award-chasing / magazine-cover-chasing.** "If you're optimizing your company to end up on the cover of a magazine, you're failing. You need customers."
- **Chasing "work-life balance" as an employee** when you want to be an entrepreneur. The two frames are fundamentally different games. Don't play one thinking you're playing the other.
- **Hyperventilating around a principle.** If you blindly inherit a maxim ("speed is everything!") without the experiential context that generated it, you just run in circles looking frantic.

## How He Builds Judgement

The practical path, as Naval actually lives it.

**1. He works.** Hard. On specific, real, difficult problems. No amount of reading substitutes for the reps. His new company is called Impossible, Inc. precisely because the problem is hard; the difficulty is the feature. He puts in what he calls "minimally retired" hours — effectively full time on something he cares about deeply. He names a co-founder who "if he's awake, he's working."

**2. He reads 1-2 hours a day — and reads to think, not to consume.** Kindle and iPad for volume; physical copies for loved books. Parallel reading, drops without guilt, skims, rereads the greats. Reads Schopenhauer's short essays, Deutsch, Borges, Ted Chiang, Taleb, Will Durant, Krishnamurti, Osho. Rereads *Skin in the Game*, *Sapiens*, *Lessons of History*, *Seven Brief Lessons in Physics*.

**3. He surrounds himself with a tiny group of peer-quality people** he'd work with for life. "I only want to be around people I know I'm going to be around with for the rest of my life." Low-ego, high-ability, intellectually curious, builders. His current company has fewer than 20 people; every one is a peer, including the 19-year-old and the 23-year-old co-founder who holds equal stock and authority.

**4. He audits his internal monologue like he's debugging code.** Catches his brain fantasy-planning a podcast answer while brushing his teeth, asks "why am I solving this problem right now?" — and puts the brain down. Treats the mind as a muscle to rest until needed. 95% of what his brain runs off trying to do doesn't need to be tackled at that moment.

**5. He loads hard problems fully into his foreground, then walks.** 24-36 hour coding sessions in college taught him that insight comes from fully loading the problem, then letting the subconscious percolate. He now does the same with business and research problems. "You can't put that on a schedule."

**6. He reflects decade by decade.** "If you do that exercise — what advice would you give your 20-year-old self, your 30-year-old, your 40-year-old — remarkable consistency emerges." His own: everything I did was right; I just did it with too much emotion and anger.

**7. He avoids the news and macro-forecasters.** Replaces them with timeless material (classics, philosophy) and bleeding-edge material (AI research, hardware, rockets, biotech).

**8. He ruthlessly cuts.** Angry people — cut. Short-term thinkers — cut. Hierarchical relationships — cut. Chores disguised as opportunities — cut. "An effort to be lazy. And lazy means not working. And not working means not doing things you don't want to do. If you want to do it, it's not work."

**9. He separates single-player from multi-player games.** Health, happiness, reading, inner work — single-player. No social affirmation, no group meditations, no cohort classes. The real work is individual and unglamorous.

**10. He lets judgement become taste, and once it does, he stops asking for outside opinions.** His investing is now almost entirely taste — on the business, on the founder, on the category, and on himself ("do I want to take a walk with this person for the next ten years?"). He hates articulating it. He doesn't need to.

**11. He tests his thinking by writing it.** Tweets, long-form conversations, podcasts. "Something affects me emotionally, then I synthesize it, I test it — is this true? — and if it's true in some interesting way, I send it out." Writing is the compression step that turns specific experience into portable general principle. Then the principle goes back into the next specific and gets updated again.

**12. He trusts his gut and doesn't second-guess it.** "It takes time to develop your gut, but once it's developed, don't listen to anything else." He's earned the right to this by decades of specific-to-general iteration.

---

The whole stack, at its simplest: **do hard things with people you respect, read an hour a day forever, reflect honestly, trust nature and markets as the only real graders, cut short-term thinking and angry people from your life, treat reversible decisions fast and irreversible ones slow, and let logic harden into intuition and intuition harden into taste.** That's the Brain of Naval on judgement.
