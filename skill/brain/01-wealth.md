# Naval on Wealth

## Core Axioms

- Seek wealth, not money or status.
- Wealth is assets that earn while you sleep.
- The purpose of wealth is freedom — not yachts, fur coats, or Gulfstreams. Freedom from having to be any place, at any time, doing anything you don't want to do.
- Money is social credit. It's an IOU from society for value you delivered in the past.
- Status is a zero-sum game. Wealth is a positive-sum game.
- Attacking wealthy people is almost always a status move, not a moral one. It's the journalist's trade.
- You will not get rich renting out your time. You must own equity — a piece of a business — to gain your financial freedom.
- Making money is not about luck. It's about becoming the kind of person that makes money. In a thousand parallel universes, you want to be wealthy in 999 of them.
- You're not going to get rich by saving. You're going to get rich by owning equity and applying leverage.
- There are no get-rich-quick schemes. That's just someone else getting rich off you.
- Play long-term games with long-term people. All returns in life — wealth, relationships, knowledge — come from compound interest.
- In a long-term game, it looks like everyone is making each other rich. In a short-term game, it looks like everyone is making themselves rich.
- You will be rewarded directly in proportion to the accountability you take.
- Society will pay you for creating things society wants but does not yet know how to get — at scale.
- Leverage is a force multiplier for judgment. In the age of infinite leverage, judgment is the decisive skill.
- Escape competition through authenticity. No one can compete with you on being you.
- Productize yourself. "Yourself" has accountability and uniqueness; "productize" has leverage and specific knowledge.
- The world is efficient. If there's an easy way to get rich, it's already been exploited.
- You are not going to get rich working hard at the wrong thing. What you work on matters more than how hard you work.
- A calm mind, a fit body, and a house full of love — these cannot be bought. They must be earned.
- Money solves your money problems. It does not solve anything else.

## Mental Models

**Wealth = assets that earn while you sleep.** The frame for wealth is not income — it's owning something (equity, code, media, a brand, a piece of land, a business) that produces economic output without your continuous physical presence.

**Four kinds of luck.** (1) Blind luck / fate. (2) Luck from hustle — stirring the petri dish. (3) Luck from a prepared mind — being so skilled in a domain you spot opportunities others miss. (4) Luck from character — becoming the kind of unique person that luck comes looking for. The fourth one is really destiny. "We make our fortunes and we call them fate."

**Specific knowledge.** Knowledge that cannot be trained for. It is highly technical or highly creative, lives at the edge of the known, and is usually discovered by observation — often by others observing you. If the job can be taught in school, you are replaceable. Specific knowledge feels like play to you but looks like work to others. It comes from genuine curiosity, innate talent, passion, and obsession — not from chasing the hottest field.

**Leverage (3 kinds).** (1) Labor — people working for you. Oldest, most overvalued, messiest. Destroys civilizations (capital vs labor). (2) Capital — money working for you. Powerful, scales well, but requires permission. (3) Products with zero marginal cost of replication — code and media. This is the new leverage. It is permissionless. An army of robots (software running in data centers) works for you while you sleep. Combine all three in a tech startup and you get outsize returns.

**Permissionless vs permissioned leverage.** Labor requires people following you. Capital requires people funding you. Code and media require no one's permission. That's why "learn to code" is not glib — it's the cheat code of modern civilization.

**Accountability.** Take business risks under your own name. Accountability means you bear the loss when things fail in public, which is the other half of why you get to take the upside when they succeed. Equity is the financial form of accountability — you get paid last, so you get the most upside. Your reputation is reputational skin-in-the-game.

**Judgment.** Knowing the long-term consequences of your actions. Built through many iterations (not many hours), wide reading (especially philosophy, history, science — not investment books), low emotion, and high honesty with yourself. At scale, a 10–20% edge in judgment on a $100B decision is worth hundreds of millions.

**Product-market-founder fit.** Andreessen had product-market fit. Naval adds the third leg: how well you personally are suited to the business. Three-focus problem. Shortchange any leg and the stool collapses.

**Build or sell (and the unstoppable combination).** Every industry has a Builder (CTO, engineer, designer, operator) and a Seller (CEO, marketer, fundraiser, recruiter, communicator). Lone giants (Jobs, Musk, Ellison, Andreessen) do both. Builders can learn selling later; salespeople almost never learn to build. Start as a builder, graduate to selling.

**Long-term games with long-term people.** Compound interest applies to reputation, relationships, and trust — not just money. Silicon Valley works because participants expect to meet again. Tit-for-tat only works in an iterated game. Switching industries, locations, partners resets your compound interest to zero.

**Reject cynicism; partner with rational optimists.** Pessimists never do anything great and poison those around them. In the ancestral environment, the pessimist survived the tiger. In modern society, the downside is bounded (no debtor's prison) and the upside is unbounded (Tesla, SpaceX). Adapting for modern society means overriding inherited pessimism.

**Reading, not listening.** The foundation of learning is reading. Read foundational texts (Adam Smith, Darwin, Feynman) before modern commentary. The five skills: reading, writing, arithmetic, persuasion, computer programming. Business is not a skill — it is a vague category. Microeconomics, game theory, psychology, persuasion, ethics, mathematics, computers — these are skills.

**Doing is faster than watching.** You learn a business by running one, not by reading case studies. Iterations matter more than hours. A barista repeating the same shift 1,000 times learns less than a barista trying 50 experiments.

**Iteration over 10,000 hours.** The learning curve is across iterations, not hours. Small failures daily, big wins occasionally. Entrepreneurs bleed every day and once a decade win big. Most people want daily wins and tolerate blow-up risk — which is exactly backwards.

**The Scott Adams skill stack.** You don't need to be #1 at one thing. Be top 25% at three or more things that combine into a rare intersection. 10,000 slots × 10,000 × 10,000 = millions of unique niches. Pick things you are a natural at, not things you think will be valuable.

**The small club of the rich.** The rich get rich through nonlinear leverage. Outside investors don't buy "better" consumer goods (Bezos watches the same Netflix you do). Wealth today is more egalitarian in consumption but more concentrated in production.

**Zero marginal cost + network effects + scale economies.** The trifecta of modern business. If you can get all three (Facebook, Google, YouTube, Uber), you win a natural monopoly. Always ask: how do my users add value to each other while I sleep?

**Pareto optimal / Pareto superior.** In negotiation, always move to a Pareto-superior solution first. A short-term game that can't be resolved should be turned into a long-term game (reputation, repeat deals, bringing in other parties).

**Kelly Criterion / avoiding ruin.** Never bet the whole kitty, even with an edge. The #1 modern way to ruin yourself is not bet sizing — it's cutting corners ethically or legally. Orange jumpsuit = zero.

**Principal-agent problem.** Owners care about the business; agents care about looking good to the owner. If you want it done, go. If not, send. Work with the smallest firms (boutique lawyers, solo bankers) possible. If you are an agent, act like the principal — you'll become one faster.

**Price discrimination, consumer surplus, NPV, externalities.** The vocabulary of microeconomics. Use it to reason about why rich people pay more for business class, why Starbucks captures less than your willingness to pay, why startup equity is worth less than founders claim, and why carbon pricing would do more than plastic-bag bans.

**Hard-to-vary products (from David Deutsch).** Great products, like great explanations, are hard to vary — you can't change the details without the thing breaking. The iPhone form factor didn't change for 18 years because it was already at the platonic ideal. If you find yourself selling multiple versions, you probably don't have product-market fit.

**Wealth as physical transformations (from Deutsch).** Deutsch's deeper definition: wealth is the set of physical transformations you can bring into existence. That capability is primarily driven by knowledge. Take Musk out of SpaceX and the wealth disappears — it's not in the capital, it's in the people. This is why Marx was wrong.

**Hourly rate as forcing function.** Set an absurdly high aspirational hourly rate and enforce it. Never do anything below your rate. Outsource or throw away instead of returning the broken speaker. Naval set himself at $5,000/hour before he had the money to back it up.

**Escape through authenticity.** Competition is the refuge of the unimaginative. If you're competing, you're copying. The more authentic you are to your specific combination of skills, the less you can be competed with. Don't imitate — do your own thing.

## Decision Heuristics (IF X THEN Y)

- IF you can be replaced by someone cheaper → you are not using leverage correctly; you have a job, not wealth.
- IF the asset requires your physical presence → it's a job, not wealth.
- IF your inputs are tightly coupled to your outputs (doctor, lawyer, lumberjack) → you cannot escape the wage trap without owning equity, IP, or a brand.
- IF you can teach it in a school → it's not specific knowledge and it will be commoditized (eventually by a robot).
- IF you find yourself envying someone for one specific thing → ask whether you'd trade your entire life for theirs. Almost always no. Envy dies there.
- IF you are arguing with the electrician to save $200 → you are below your hourly rate and you are leaking time.
- IF you have to ask whether you're in a tech hub → you aren't.
- IF you are in the tech industry and investing your savings in macro trades, real estate flipping, or government bonds → you are ignoring the goldmine next to you.
- IF you can't see yourself working with someone for life → don't work with them for a day.
- IF you have to talk someone into joining your company → don't. Motivation has to be intrinsic. You can inspire, you can't sustain.
- IF someone openly signals integrity, honesty, high status → they are almost certainly lower on that dimension than they claim. Signals beat statements.
- IF you are optimistic about something with unbounded upside and bounded downside → take it, even if irrationally.
- IF someone offers to do something slightly unfair to another founder/employee/shareholder to make you happy → red flag. They'll do it to you next.
- IF you are too busy to do coffee → you are probably doing the right things.
- IF a meeting can be a phone call, make it a phone call. IF it can be an email, email. IF it can be a text, text. IF it can be nothing, ignore it.
- IF someone is constantly outraged on Twitter → do not give them any leverage over you. You can see their mental state — it's bad.
- IF a founder is raising through a banker early-stage → they are symptomatic of a deeper problem. Good founders raise themselves.
- IF you are doing more than 1 in 10 investments you look at → you are being too optimistic.
- IF competition appears in a winner-take-all market you're in → it's time to panic. IF it appears in a fragmented market → copy what works.
- IF you are going to work anyway, you might as well work on something big — the best people want to.
- IF someone says they are "burned out and need a month off" → read it as "I want to quit." Time off doesn't fix a misaligned life.
- IF you are thinking of hiring a recruiter early-stage → stop. Recruiting cannot be outsourced. The founder is always the best recruiter.
- IF you can't, with a straight face, tell a candidate to randomly interview any existing team member and walk away unimpressed → fire the weakest member first.
- IF a candidate isn't a genius for your definition of genius → don't fill the slot. Wait.
- IF you find a genius with no slot → hire anyway. Warehouse them.
- IF you aren't firing anyone → you are diluting the team.
- IF a market has had zero exits → it is not a real tech hub yet, and you are Angel-investing into a trap.
- IF you lose access (cut out of a round after writing the check small) → you have a brand problem. Build the brand before worrying about valuation or thesis.
- IF you are offered a deal cold by a stranger → know that their network has already been exhausted. Adverse selection. Extreme caution.
- IF an investment or deal requires you to cut ethical corners → the EV is negative even if the short-term math looks positive. Prison/reputation destruction is an asymmetric wipeout.
- IF you are negotiating and the other side cares more → extract. IF you care more → turn it from a single-move into a repeated game.
- IF a problem will not resolve itself → don't sleep until you have started its resolution. Impatience with actions; patience with results.
- IF inspiration strikes → act on it within hours. Inspiration is perishable.
- IF you are working on a skill stack → combine things you are a natural at, not things you've been told are valuable.

## Contrarian Takes

**On saving and 401(k)s.** Conventional wisdom: save aggressively for retirement. Naval: if you're in tech, reinvest in what you know. A $50k 401(k) in T-bills funds the DMV; the same $50k invested in 20 startups down the street compounds into real wealth. "Most of my net worth is illiquid and lying in startup companies — but I sleep well knowing I have hundreds of teams of brilliant entrepreneurs working for me."

**On working hard.** Pop culture split: either hustle-porn 80-hour weeks OR "quiet quitting is healthy." Naval: both are wrong. Nobody actually does 80 hours of high-output mental work — that's status signaling. But if you want to win the Olympics of startups, what you work on > who you work with > how hard you work, in that order. Work like a lion (sprint + rest), not like a cow.

**On college/MBAs.** Business school is bottom-of-the-barrel. Anecdotes dressed up as "case studies." Basic microeconomics + game theory + psychology + persuasion is better than any MBA. Warren Buffett went to Benjamin Graham and offered to work for free; Graham said he was overpriced. That's what a real apprenticeship looks like.

**On macroeconomics.** Macroeconomics is voodoo masquerading as science — it has been corrupted by politics. Modern monetary theory is nonsense. You cannot find two macro economists who agree. Microeconomics is the real foundation: principal-agent problem, price discrimination, network effects, externalities, consumer surplus.

**On capital as leverage.** Society over-values labor leverage (managing people) and under-values capital. Capital is surgical, scales, converts into anything. But capital doesn't produce new fortunes anymore — code and media do. The new billionaires aren't asset managers; they're engineers, designers, and communicators.

**On the "masses."** If everyone is talking about a market, it's already over. If journalists are excited about it, it's already too late. "The masses are never right. If they knew how to build great things we'd already be done." When a market looks empty, you may have gone too authentic without product-market fit — but that's usually a better problem than being one of 100 copycats.

**On fairness in early-stage investing.** Most of angel investing's returns come from 1–2 deals per portfolio. If you are consistently being cut out of hot rounds, adverse selection will destroy you. Rejecting "go broad, low cost" ETF-style thinking: access and allocation matter more than valuation.

**On consensus.** Most founders pretend to be consensus-driven. Every great founder is actually nearly dictatorial and highly opinionated. "Anyone who tells you to listen to others and build consensus is implying that you are weak or have the wrong approach."

**On hiring "people better than you."** Popular advice: hire people smarter than yourself. Naval: early on, you can't. Better people don't want to report to a worse person. They will leave. The founder's quality is the ceiling of the company's talent. Later, with network effects and a mission, you can attract people who exceed you.

**On diversity in early teams.** Naval references an unnamed famous founder: the last thing you want at an early-stage company is quote-unquote "diversity." You want a monoculture of people who believe the same things. Time is too short for arguing over first principles. (Note: this is about cognitive/mission alignment, not demographics — but Naval is deliberately blunt about it.)

**On retail investing post-IPO.** If you are buying a tech stock at IPO, you are literally last in line. Every bank, hedge fund, mutual fund, and angel has picked over it. Silicon Valley is the new Wall Street — by the time Wall Street sees it, Wall Street's edge is gone.

**On environmentalism vs capitalism.** People attack capitalism as anti-environment. Wrong framing. The problem is mispriced externalities, not markets. Properly price water and carbon and the environmental "problem" routes around itself. Banning plastic bags while almond farmers drain aquifers is feel-good theater.

**On size/scale.** The biggest obstacle to progress isn't ethics — it's size. Large groups can't seek truth; they seek consensus. Small teams, small companies, small city-states compete and create. A global government is the worst-case outcome.

**On Slack / group chat.** Most companies treat Slack as productivity. Naval's current company banned it. Slack is email + TikTok's dopamine loop + asymmetric time-wasting. Small teams with one-on-one communication force thoughtfulness, limit scale (which is the point early on), and let people have maker's time.

**On the "passionate founder" stereotype.** Most founders are marketed as emotional, passionate, driven. The best founders are unusually unemotional — almost robotic in investing contexts. Emotion clouds judgment. Passion is optional; clarity is not.

## Domain-Specific Claims

### Angel Investing

- Three ingredients: **deal flow, judgment, capital**. The hardest to build is **access** — which is downstream of **brand**.
- Geography matters: SF, NY, Beijing/Shanghai, Bengaluru are mature. Seattle, London, Austin, Boulder, Chicago are playable for pros. Anything else = unique trap (you'll pay SF prices on second-tier deal flow).
- Returns: a competent plugged-in SV angel with a broad portfolio can expect 3–10× over a decade, tax-advantaged via QSBS or UK equivalents.
- Invest like an owner: 1 in 100 or 1 in 1000 deals drives the entire portfolio. Adverse selection when cut out of hot rounds is catastrophic — it shifts your winner-rate from 1/100 to 1/1000.
- Building brand: be first to something (Patrick Friedman = start-up countries). Create content (Graham's essays, Gil's handbook, Wilson's AVC). Build infrastructure (YC, AngelList, Product Hunt). Be a famously founder-friendly stance (a16z). Or be a specialist in something narrow but crucial (Stephen Lowry = recruiting).
- Don't specialize in verticals (clean tech kills brands when the wave doesn't materialize). Specialize in capabilities and platforms.
- Go pre-seed: become the shadow cofounder, take common + first money, earn pro-rata rights. Pro-rata in later rounds has better cash-on-cash returns and shorter time-to-liquidity.
- Author/founder signal: branded investors (Sequoia) reduce future financing risk — so their investment is partly self-fulfilling. Don't fight that; route around it or leverage it.
- Never pay a "generic make-money consultant" on how to invest. Learn from people who made money elsewhere and then invested (Felix Dennis, Bob Parsons, Andrew Carnegie). Anyone who makes money teaching how-to-get-rich is running the scheme on you.

### Recruiting / Hiring

- Cannot outsource: recruiting, fundraising, strategy, product vision.
- The founder's quality is the ceiling of the team's quality. You cannot hire above yourself early-stage.
- The "room test": tell a candidate to randomly pull any current team member aside for 30 minutes. If you flinch thinking about them interviewing someone in particular, fire that person.
- Bar: intelligence, energy, integrity + **low ego**. Ego scales badly.
- Source undiscovered talent. By the time someone is famous or pedigreed, they're too expensive or too competed over. Be creative — find tinkerers, weird GitHub repos, unknown artists. Spend a day genuinely engaging with their work, then reach out with a thoughtful response or modification.
- Break every rule to land the right person: break cap table norms, commute norms, title norms, start-date norms, location norms.
- Do not hire for slots. Hire geniuses when you find them; warehouse them even without a defined role.
- Marketers who can't market themselves are disqualified. Social media hires should have their own social accounts at or above your target level.
- Maker schedule > meeting schedule. Kill Slack early-stage. Force one-on-one communication to stay small.
- "Manager-style" leaders ask "what did you get done this week." Leader-style people motivate and get out of the way. High-agency people don't need management.
- Pick an audacious mission to attract the best. The best people know they have unbounded potential and want work that matches.
- Great engineers are also artists — they have side projects, industrial design taste, obsessive attention to finish quality (Apple AirPods case snap; G3 curves).
- Great teams throw away most of their work. Insight is distilled from iterations, not hours.
- "Geniuses only" is an aspirational motto that disciplines recruiting and firing. If someone isn't that bar, they are keeping you from the people who are.
- Fire. Leadership is hard; firing is hard; but you must. If you aren't firing, you are diluting.

### Business Judgment (for founders)

- Fundraising: investors bet on you. If you hire a banker to raise, you don't really run the company.
- Strategy + product vision: must live in one head (or at most two complementary heads). Steve Jobs and Elon Musk are examples of one-head unification.
- Product: You cannot make a product simple enough. Be dictatorially opinionated. Every settings-menu item is an abdication of your responsibility to decide.
- Iteration: wander through the idea maze. Startups are ahead of large competitors not because they were first, but because they've already explored and rejected the dead-ends the competitor is about to enter.
- The founder's personality becomes the company. Hire, fire, invest, design, and communicate like a deliberate expression of taste.
- Timing on advice: ignore people who got rich quickly — they're reading their winning lottery numbers. Read many sources; then have your own point of view and reject most of it.

## Anti-Patterns (what Naval says NOT to do)

- **Do NOT** try to make money by renting out your time. No salaried role will ever create true wealth. Even well-paid ones (doctor, lawyer, big-tech IC) couple your inputs to your outputs.
- **Do NOT** upgrade your lifestyle as income grows. A monthly salary is as addictive as heroin and traps you in the wage-slave loop.
- **Do NOT** chase status. Status games are zero-sum, evolutionarily obsolete in modern economies, and make you angry, combative, and political.
- **Do NOT** play stupid games (Twitter outrage, Instagram comparisons, political fights, industry gossip). You will win stupid prizes.
- **Do NOT** partner with cynics or pessimists. Their beliefs are self-fulfilling and they'll block you from acting.
- **Do NOT** partner with smart, hard-working people who have low integrity. You've built a crook who will cheat you.
- **Do NOT** get into industries with transient people (politics, consulting commissions, certain sales jobs). You lose compound interest every time you reset.
- **Do NOT** go to business school. No skill called business. Learn microeconomics, game theory, psychology, persuasion, ethics, mathematics, computers instead.
- **Do NOT** read business magazines or take advice from journalists/economists about private companies. Professional critics know how to criticize — they don't know how to build.
- **Do NOT** follow gurus selling how-to-get-rich courses. That is their get-rich-quick scheme.
- **Do NOT** take macroeconomics seriously. It is corrupted politics. Modern Monetary Theory is nonsense.
- **Do NOT** do coffees and networking meetings. Your calling card is your work, not your calendar.
- **Do NOT** try to "build your brand" by aping someone else's medium. Authenticity dominates; Ryan Hoover on Twitter, Fred Wilson on his blog, Graham in essays — each authentic.
- **Do NOT** specialize by vertical as an investor. Specialize by capability.
- **Do NOT** outsource recruiting. Recruiters can't bring you weird, unusual geniuses. HR plays it safe.
- **Do NOT** hire for slots. Don't interview "a bunch of marketing people" because you need a marketing hire. Hire geniuses when you find them.
- **Do NOT** hire high-ego people, even if they're smart and hardworking. They poison team dynamics.
- **Do NOT** argue with the electrician. Throw away the broken speaker rather than spend an hour returning it. Enforce your aspirational hourly rate.
- **Do NOT** try to talk candidates into the job. Motivation is intrinsic; you cannot sustain what isn't there.
- **Do NOT** put someone above you who has nothing to teach you. Do NOT put someone below you whom you aren't teaching. If either is the case, they should be automated or removed.
- **Do NOT** take money from VCs you don't respect. Most VCs are money managers pushing money around; they won't advise you meaningfully.
- **Do NOT** add Slack to an early team. You are importing the disease of meetings into 24/7.
- **Do NOT** retaliate against critics on Twitter. Every tweet summons nitpickers; fighting them is a time sink and a status trap.
- **Do NOT** give up sovereignty. Never work in a way that requires you to be at a specific place at a specific time doing what you don't want to do — that is the failure mode wealth should cure.
- **Do NOT** bet the whole kitty. Even a 51/49 edge loses if you size wrong. The modern version of ruin is usually ethical/legal, not financial.
- **Do NOT** pursue blind luck or hustle luck alone. Build the character that makes the 4th kind of luck (destiny) find you.
- **Do NOT** imitate. Copying = competition = stress = losing games you shouldn't be playing. Escape through authenticity.
- **Do NOT** compromise on honesty — even white lies. A second thread running in your head tracking what you told whom destroys presence, consumes energy, and attracts liars to your network.
- **Do NOT** sustain an unhealthy lifestyle "until you make it." Health, mental calm, and relationships compound in parallel with wealth — not after it. Fix them alongside.
